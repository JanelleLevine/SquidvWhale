Squid vs Whale

02

Every day, the hunt begins

Off the coast of Spain, researchers track a pod led by Sirene, a mature sperm whale. They use both advanced cameras and a simple suction-cup pole to attach sensors and hear whale clicks during the hunt.

How long can you hold your breath? A sperm whale can stay down for 60–90 minutes.

03

Into the twilight zone

Sirene dives into the dark, where pressure is high and light is nearly gone. The pod hunts giant squid, animals built to evade with jet propulsion, color change, ink clouds, and enormous eyes.

Are you afraid of the dark? How do animals find their way when there is almost no light?

04

A tag, a hunt, a surprise

The team’s suction-cup camera finally sticks to Sirene. She dives with her pod, clicks ring out, and the scientists watch the beginning of a hunt unfold in a far darker world than any sailor ever imagined.

05

The science of the hunt

Scientists now know this chase is an evolutionary arms race. Sperm whales use sonar and family culture, while giant squid use speed, color change, ink, and sharp suckers. Every adaptation is a response to the other.

Which animal do you think has the harder job?

06

Because of that, Sirene taught the pod

Sirene is older and nearly toothless, yet she still leads the hunt. She relies on years of knowledge, calm patience, and her pod’s communication to teach younger whales how to hunt in the deep.

Why is experience as important as strength?

07

Because of that, the ocean changed

These interactions shape the twilight food web. Whale falls feed scavengers, squid vanish into darkness, and every connection matters in this hidden ecosystem.

08

And ever since then

This is a story about discovery, not monsters. It is about human curiosity, new tools, and the responsibility to protect the deep-ocean world that connects whales, squid, and people.
